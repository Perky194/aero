Dairy Industry Act (Cap. 336)

Enacted in 1958 (updated 2022), this foundational law establishes the Kenya Dairy Board, mandates inspection, licensing, and oversight of all dairy operators :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}. Farmers and processors must register and comply with standards or face penalties.

Dairy Produce Safety Regulations (Legal Notice 22/2021)

These regulations enforce food safety across the dairy value chain — covering sanitation, equipment standards, labelling, sampling, disposal, and hygienic transport :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}.

Registration, Licensing & Levies (Legal Notice 16/2021)

All milk handlers must obtain permits (farmer cooperatives, transporters, milk bars, mini‑dairies). The Kenya Dairy Board sets levy frameworks and cess to fund regulation :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}.

Pricing Regulations (LN 23/2021)

Sets minimum farm‑gate and bulk milk payout prices: e.g. raw unchilled milk minimum KSh 33/kg; chilled KSh 35/kg :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}. Records and transparency are legally required.

Kenya Bureau of Standards & Other Acts

KEBS (under the Standards Act Cap 496) defines specifications for milk, cheese, yoghurt, etc. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}. Other supporting laws include the Public Health Act (Cap 242), Weights & Measures Act (Cap 513), Environmental Management Act and more :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}.

Why Compliance Matters

  • Ensures consumer safety — prevents adulteration & harmful residues.
  • Unlocks market access — both domestic (retail, processors) and export.
  • Lowers legal risk — non‑compliance can lead to fines, seizure, or licence suspension.
  • Improves professionalism — enhances value chain trust & investment.
